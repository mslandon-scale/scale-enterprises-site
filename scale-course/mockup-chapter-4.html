Stage 3
Enterprise Leadership
7 → 8 Figures — The stage this whole playbook was built for.

Every one of the 56 checklist elements is installed and running. The Rule of Three is in seat, the Leadership System is operating, and the cash is flowing enough to fund the next move. This is where a business becomes an enterprise and where a founder becomes an owner and true Enterprise Entrepreneur.

What This Stage Looks Like

The checklist was built for this stage. This is where a business becomes an enterprise, and where a founder becomes an owner. There are no new elements to add here; there's only the discipline of making sure the full checklist is actually in place, not just listed.

What I look at, at this stage, are three specific litmus tests. These are the numbers that tell me whether the business is truly operating at Enterprise Leadership or just pretending to.

Litmus Test #1 — Revenue Per Employee (RPE) of $250K+

$250K per employee is the baseline marker of a healthy scaling business. Note: with AI now embedded across every function, this number will keep climbing. $300K, $400K, and beyond is quickly becoming the benchmark for a genuinely competitive business. Track where yours sits, and refuse to let it slide.

Litmus Test #2 — Net Margins of 20–25%

If net margins aren't hitting 20–25%, I treat that as a signal to go back to the drawing board — not to hustle harder. I look at the offer. I look at the value we're creating in the marketplace. I look at whether we're targeting the right customers, or whether we've drifted into serving lower-value customers. I look at team efficiency and labor cost per unit. I keep refining until the margins land where they need to for long-term scalability.

Litmus Test #3 — Founder's Time

At this stage, my number one goal is to have the ability to not be in the business anymore. Not necessarily to leave, but to have the choice. I need to be able to step away for 30 days, six months, a full year, and watch the business keep running and keep growing without me. I need to be able to sit on the board as Chairman instead of CEO or COO if I want to. I can still choose to be those things; the point is that I have the option. If I don't have the option, the business still owns me, and I haven't actually reached Enterprise Leadership.

Stage 4
Platform
8 → 9 Figures — Every major function, in-house.

You keep everything from the checklist, add middle management beneath the leadership team, and dominate all customer acquisition channels in parallel. Every major business function — marketing, leadership, finance, tech, operations, people, sales strategy, and legal — gets brought in-house. The business becomes fully vertically integrated, genuinely self-contained, and completely under your control.

What This Stage Looks Like

Platform is where the business stops being something you run and becomes something that runs itself, with you AND most of your leadership team overseeing strategy. Everything on the checklist stays in place, gets optimized, and it all compounds.

Up until this point, most operators have been holding on to the marketing, or at the very least the delivery — making sure there's actually something of value to offer the market. Everything else — finance, legal, HR, tech, even parts of operations — often gets outsourced to agencies, CPA firms, outside counsel, and third-party providers. That's the right move early. It's the wrong move at this stage.

The Platform stage is where you bring all of it under one roof. You stop hiring the marketing agency and build a marketing team. You stop renting the CPA firm and hire a real CFO with a finance department behind them. You stop calling outside legal counsel for every contract and build an in-house legal function. You stop outsourcing HR and build a real people operation. The business becomes vertically integrated, fully compliant, and genuinely self-contained — every core function owned and controlled internally.

Alongside all of this, middle management gets installed beneath the executive leadership team. That layer is what frees the leadership team to focus on strategy rather than execution. Without it, the leadership team ends up doing management work and the business stalls right at the edge of Platform.

On the acquisition side, Stage 1 was about picking one channel. Stage 2 was about refining it. Stage 3 was about expanding channels. Stage 4 is about dominating all of them — digital marketing/funnels, prospecting, referrals, partners and affiliates, traditional and guerrilla, all running in parallel, each with its own dedicated team and budget.

The Linchpin

Reaching Platform status is the linchpin to billionaire-style success and being an owner of real capital. It's also the stage where the moves I'm about to describe in Stage 5 become available. Which brings us to the endgame.

Stage 5
Private Equity
9 → 10 Figures — Pure strategy, pure leverage.

By the time you're here, the operating business runs itself. Your job shifts entirely to strategy — roll-ups, M&A, franchising, vertical integration, and acquiring competitors. It becomes a game of deal flow, capitalization, and integration. This is also where the wealth being generated has to be protected and managed separately from the business itself.

What This Stage Looks Like

A Note From Me

I definitely haven't personally reached this stage yet. What's on this page is drawn from studying the operators who have — not from firsthand execution. Right now I'm focused on consolidating activities that lead toward a significant exit of my own. If you want to dig deeper into what this stage actually looks like in practice, one of the best resources I've found is PitchBook — a tool where you can study real valuations, real exits, and what's actually selling in any given category.

Private Equity is the stage where the operating business runs itself and the operator's job shifts entirely to strategy. At this point the game is no longer about building the business — it's about using the business you've built as a platform for deploying capital. This is where serial entrepreneurs get to have all of the fun!

The strategic moves at this stage are roll-ups of smaller operators in your category, strategic M&A that adds capability or customer base, franchising the model out to operators who run it under your brand, vertical integration into suppliers and distributors, and acquiring competitors outright. Done well, any one of these can add more market share in a single deal than a year of organic growth. This is where billionaires are made.

The Three Functions That Matter at This Stage
Deal Flow — a consistent pipeline of acquisition targets
Capitalization — access to debt, equity, and strategic capital on demand
Integration — a repeatable playbook for absorbing acquired companies without breaking them

Protecting the Wealth That's Being Created

The wealth being generated at this stage has to be protected, and it has to be handled separately from the operating business. I'd have my personal wealth, estate, and tax strategy fully built out and running by the time I reached here — not scrambling to set it up. The structure I'd be looking at to manage all of that outside the operating company is a Family Office. That's the move I'd make to ensure the wealth compounds in its own right, independent of whatever happens next with the business.
